# Lab 01: Building Our AI 100 Code of Conduct

**Total time:** ~40 minutes

## Purpose

AI 100 asks students to discuss topics that can be controversial, personal, or uncomfortable: surveillance, discrimination, mental health, misinformation, sexuality, politics, religion, war, labour, creativity, and harm. The goal of this lab is to create shared expectations for how we will talk, disagree, give feedback, and support each other while studying those topics.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab, students should be able to:

- **Identify** behaviours that support respectful discussion in an AI ethics course.
- **Explain** why sensitive AI topics may affect labmates differently.
- **Apply** shared discussion norms to realistic classroom and group-work scenarios.
- **Create** draft principles for an AI 100 code of conduct.
- **Reflect** on when to pause a conversation, speak with a peer, or reach out to the teaching team for support.

## Materials

- Shared document, whiteboard, or chart paper
- Sticky notes or index cards (physical), or a shared Miro board (digital)
- Timer
- [Possible Code of Conduct](code-of-conduct.md) for TA reference
- [Lab 01 slides](../../slides/discussion-01_slides.qmd) for the opening and activity prompts

## Facilitation Notes for TAs

The code of conduct should not be delivered as a finished policy for students to passively accept. Students should generate the principles themselves. Use the possible code of conduct as a steering tool: listen for student language, help groups sharpen vague principles, and connect their ideas to the course's expectations.

When students discuss discomfort or conflict, do not imply that they are required to confront another person directly. A reasonable pathway is:

1. Respectfully pause the conversation when something feels off.
2. If comfortable and safe, talk with the person or group about the concern.
3. Reach out to the TA or instructor for support, especially when the issue involves harm, repeated behaviour, power dynamics, personal disclosure, or uncertainty about what to do.

Emphasize that dialogue skills improve through practice and reflection, and that this course is a valuable opportunity to grow in these communication skills.

Throughout, help students notice that the same AI topic can affect labmates very differently depending on their backgrounds and experiences — naming this is part of why the lab needs shared norms. Treat the step timings and on-screen timers as flexible guides, and adjust them to your section.

### Collecting Student Input

- Students post ideas on physical sticky notes or a shared Miro board, then cluster similar ideas into themes. 
- Choose the collection method with privacy in mind: physical notes can stay anonymous, while a digital board may attach names. 
- During the merge step, a TA can use an approved AI tool (e.g., UBC MS Copilot) to compile and de-duplicate the posted principles into a single draft code of conduct instead of transcribing by hand. 
- [Accessible Input] Offer an accessible alternative to writing on sticky notes or a visual board: students may type into a shared document, or dictate their ideas to a partner or TA who writes them down. No one should be excluded by the input method due to their abilities or comfort level.

## Opening (delivered via slides)

The opening logistics and framing are delivered with the [Lab 01 slides](../../slides/discussion-01_slides.qmd) (~5 minutes): TA introductions, what labs are for, participation expectations, how to get help in and out of lab, the suggestions mechanism for course feedback, and a short "why a code of conduct?" framing. The in-lab activity below begins once that framing is done.

### 1. Individual Reflection, 5 minutes

Students write on sticky notes (physical or Miro) by themselves:

> Think of a time when a group discussion went well, or when it went badly — for example, in a high school lab, a club, a team, or an online community. What made the difference?

Ask students to write down (one idea per sticky note):

- One behaviour or rule that helped
- One behaviour or rule that harmed trust or participation
- One principle they would want in this course

Students do not need to share personal details. **Keep these sticky notes — they feed directly into Step 2.**

### 2. Small-Group Drafting, 10 minutes

In groups of 3–4, students bring the sticky notes they just wrote (no need to rewrite them). As a group:

- Cluster similar behaviours and rules into themes.
- Turn each cluster into a principle for the AI 100 code of conduct (aim for 3–5 per group).

Their principles should address:

- How we discuss sensitive AI topics
- How we disagree with ideas without attacking people
- How we give and receive feedback
- How we handle jokes, sarcasm, or dismissive comments about serious issues
- What to do when someone feels uncomfortable
- When to involve a TA or instructor

Guidance for students:

- Use clear language.
- Make the principle actionable.
- Prefer specifics over vague statements like "be respectful" — but aim for a balance: specific enough to guide action, general enough to cover situations no one anticipated. Even real-world codes of conduct rely on some general language and, ultimately, on common sense.
- Write principles that would help someone know what to do in a difficult moment.

### 3. Merge into One Lab Code of Conduct, 8 minutes

Bring the groups together and build a single lab code of conduct using a rotating share-out:

- Going around the room, each group volunteers **one** principle that is not already on the board.
- Continue rotating until all distinct principles have been captured, skipping duplicates.
- A TA records the combined list in real time, and may use an AI tool to compile and de-duplicate (review the output before sharing).

Groups can nominate a speaker, take turns, or simply add a principle to the shared board or document in writing — no student is required to speak in front of the whole lab.

The result is one draft lab code of conduct that the whole room has contributed to.

### 4. Scenario Testing (whole lab), 8 minutes

As a lab, test the merged code against two or three of the scenarios below — choose the ones most relevant to your section, and cover more only if time allows. For each scenario, ask:

- Which principle applies?
- Is our principle clear enough to guide action?
- What should the student, group, TA, or instructor do next?
- Does our code need revision?

These scenarios are in the spirit of workplace-conduct training. For real-world examples, see the [UBC Bullying & Harassment resources](https://bullyingandharassment.ubc.ca/resources/) (originally developed by WorkSafe BC).

#### Scenario A: Dismissive Comment

During a discussion about algorithmic bias, a student says, "People are too sensitive about this stuff. It's just technology."

#### Scenario B: Joking About Sensitive Topics

A group is discussing AI-generated images and someone makes a joke about sexualized deepfakes. A classmate gets quiet and stops participating.

#### Scenario C: Disagreement Over Harmful AI Uses

A student argues that a surveillance tool could be useful if it improves safety. Another student says the idea is harmful and should not be discussed at all.

#### Scenario G: Fear of Saying the Wrong Thing

During a discussion about whether AI companies should be allowed to train models on copyrighted works, several students have different opinions but stay silent. One student wants to argue that there may be ethical tradeoffs worth discussing, but worries others will see the view as harmful. The conversation becomes cautious and superficial, with few students willing to disagree openly.

### 5. Wrap-up and Finalize (whole lab), 4 minutes

As a lab, fold in any revisions the scenarios surfaced. After lab, the teaching team consolidates the lab code of conduct — plus any important ideas that were missed — into a single unified AI 100 code of conduct, which is then published for the lab.

### Exit ticket (PrairieLearn, asynchronous)

Students complete a short individual reflection in PrairieLearn after lab (see [exit-ticket-spec.md](exit-ticket-spec.md)):

> What is one responsibility you have when discussing sensitive AI ethics topics in this course?

## Scenario Bank (additional scenarios for reuse or other sections)

These scenarios are not part of the core 40-minute lab, but are kept for reuse — for example, to give different discussion sections different scenarios, or for sections that have extra time.

#### Scenario D: Personal Disclosure

During a discussion about mental health chatbots, a student shares a personal experience with anxiety. The group is unsure how to respond.

#### Scenario E: Criticism of a Peer's Project Idea

One student says another group's project idea is "obviously unethical." The other group feels attacked and becomes defensive.

#### Scenario F: When to Ask for Help

A student feels uncomfortable with the direction of their group's conversation but is not sure whether the concern is serious enough to bring to a TA.

## TA Debrief After Lab

After lab, TAs should briefly compare notes:

- Which principles appeared across groups?
- Which scenarios generated the most uncertainty?
- Did students raise concerns that should inform the official course code of conduct?
- Were any project-boundary questions raised that should move to the project guidelines?
