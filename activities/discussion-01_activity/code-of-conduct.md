# Code of Conduct

This code of conduct serves as a reference for TAs and the teaching team. In Lab 01, students will attempt to develop their own principles first. Use this to steer discussion, identify missing areas, and synthesize common themes after students have done the work.

## Purpose

AI 100 is a course about the social, technical, ethical, political, and human impacts of AI. Some topics may be controversial, sensitive, or personally meaningful. We can discuss difficult ideas, but we need shared expectations for how we treat each other while doing that work.

This code focuses on interpersonal communication and group dynamics. Project topic boundaries belong in the project guidelines.

## Core Principle

We critique ideas, systems, and technologies carefully. We do not attack, dismiss, or stereotype people. Productive disagreement rests on a shared commitment to truth, responsibility, and respect: we follow the evidence honestly, take ownership of our contributions, and work to acknowledge and negotiate differences rather than paper over them.

## Grounding in UBC Policy

These norms align with university expectations:

- **Respectful environment.** UBC policy treats bullying and harassment — abusive, hostile, or vexatious behaviour that creates an intimidating environment for working, learning, or living — as unacceptable ([UBC Bullying & Harassment](https://bullyingandharassment.ubc.ca/)).
- **Academic freedom.** UBC protects the freedom to consider any opinion, including unpopular or uncomfortable ones, and treats behaviour that obstructs free and full discussion as a threat to the University's academic forum ([UBC Academic Freedom](https://academic.ubc.ca/academic-community/academic-freedom)).

Difficult discussion is encouraged; personal attacks and harassment are not.

## Discussion Principles

### 1. Listen Before Responding

Make a good-faith effort to understand what someone is saying before disagreeing. Ask clarifying questions when a comment seems unclear, incomplete, or surprising.

### 2. Critique Ideas, Not People

It is appropriate to challenge claims, assumptions, evidence, and possible harms. It is not appropriate to insult classmates, assume bad intent, or treat someone's disagreement with you as a personal attack.

### 3. Take Sensitive Topics Seriously

AI ethics discussions may involve surveillance, discrimination, mental health, sexual content, misinformation, politics, religion, violence, war, labour, disability, and other serious topics. Avoid jokes, sarcasm, or casual examples that minimize harm or make classmates feel targeted.

### 4. Make Space for Different Experiences

Students will bring different backgrounds, technical experience, identities, and personal histories. Do not expect classmates to speak for a group they belong to. Do not pressure anyone to share personal experiences. Where perspectives differ, work to understand and negotiate those differences respectfully rather than dismiss them.

### 5. Handle Personal Disclosure With Care

If someone shares something personal, listen respectfully and do not turn their experience into a debate example. Keep the discussion focused on care, privacy, and consent. If the conversation becomes heavy or unsafe, pause and ask for TA support.

### 6. Give Feedback Constructively

When responding to a peer's idea or project, be specific about the concern and focus on improvement. For example: "I worry this could expose private information. What safeguard could reduce that risk?" is more useful than "This is obviously unethical."

### 7. Pause When Needed

If a conversation feels harmful, confusing, or too personal, it is acceptable to pause. A pause can sound like:

- "Can we slow down for a moment?"
- "I am uncomfortable with how this is being framed."
- "Can we ask the TA to help us think this through?"
- "I need a minute before continuing."

### 8. Seek Support Early

If comfortable and safe, students may first raise the concern directly with the person or group involved. Students are also encouraged to reach out to the TA or instructor for support, especially when the issue involves repeated behaviour, personal harm, power dynamics, sensitive disclosure, or uncertainty about what to do.

### 9. Respect Privacy

Do not share another student's personal story, concern, identity, or group conflict outside the context where it was shared unless they have given permission or there is a safety concern that needs teaching team support. Be thoughtful about your own disclosures as well: once you share personal information, you have limited control over how it travels and limited recourse if it is repeated outside the room.

### 10. Treat the Code as Revisable

Good shared norms improve through practice. If a principle is unclear, incomplete, or not helping the class handle real situations, the teaching team should revise it with student input.

## Growth Mindset

Your discussion skills do not need to be perfect. Talking with people who think, act, or feel differently can stir strong emotions, and that is normal. Treat each activity as a chance to practise the skills of healthy professional discussion — empathy, a commitment to truth, attentive listening, finding common ground, drawing careful distinctions, and acknowledging differences before moving forward. Approached this way, the course becomes valuable preparation for similar conversations you will face in your career and life.
