# Code of Conduct

This code of conduct serves as a reference for TAs and the teaching team. In Lab 1, students will attempt to develop their own principles first. Use this to steer discussion, identify missing areas, and synthesize common themes after students have done the work.

## Purpose

AI 100 is a course about the social, ethical, political, and human impacts of AI. Some topics may be controversial, sensitive, or personally meaningful. We can discuss difficult ideas, but we need shared expectations for how we treat each other while doing that work.

This code focuses on interpersonal communication and group dynamics. Project topic boundaries belong in the project guidelines.

## Core Principle

We critique ideas, systems, and technologies carefully. We do not attack, dismiss, or stereotype people.

## Discussion Principles

## 1. Listen Before Responding

Make a good-faith effort to understand what someone is saying before disagreeing. Ask clarifying questions when a comment seems unclear, incomplete, or surprising.

## 2. Critique Ideas, Not People

It is appropriate to challenge claims, assumptions, evidence, and possible harms. It is not appropriate to insult classmates, assign motives, or treat disagreement as a personal failure.

## 3. Take Sensitive Topics Seriously

AI ethics discussions may involve surveillance, discrimination, mental health, sexual content, misinformation, politics, religion, violence, war, labour, disability, and other serious topics. Avoid jokes, sarcasm, or casual examples that minimize harm or make classmates feel targeted.

## 4. Make Space for Different Experiences

Students will bring different backgrounds, technical experience, identities, and personal histories. Do not expect classmates to speak for a group they belong to. Do not pressure anyone to share personal experiences.

## 5. Handle Personal Disclosure With Care

If someone shares something personal, listen respectfully and do not turn their experience into a debate example. Keep the discussion focused on care, privacy, and consent. If the conversation becomes heavy or unsafe, pause and ask for TA support.

## 6. Give Feedback Constructively

When responding to a peer's idea or project, be specific about the concern and focus on improvement. For example: "I worry this could expose private information. What safeguard could reduce that risk?" is more useful than "This is obviously unethical."

## 7. Pause When Needed

If a conversation feels harmful, confusing, or too personal, it is acceptable to pause. A pause can sound like:

- "Can we slow down for a moment?"
- "I am uncomfortable with how this is being framed."
- "Can we ask the TA to help us think this through?"
- "I need a minute before continuing."

## 8. Seek Support Early

If comfortable and safe, students may first talk with the person or group involved. Students are also encouraged to reach out to the TA or instructor for support, especially when the issue involves repeated behaviour, personal harm, power dynamics, sensitive disclosure, or uncertainty about what to do.

## 9. Respect Privacy

Do not share another student's personal story, concern, identity, or group conflict outside the context where it was shared unless they have given permission or there is a safety concern that needs teaching team support.

## 10. Treat the Code as Revisable

Good shared norms improve through practice. If a principle is unclear, incomplete, or not helping the class handle real situations, the teaching team should revise it with student input.
